## What the paper establishes

Chan, Golub, and LeVeque provide a rigorous numerical analysis of algorithms for computing sample variance. Their contributions include:

1. **Error analysis of Welford's algorithm:** They prove that Welford's one-pass formula has relative error bounded by O(n ε), where ε is machine epsilon — the same asymptotic bound as the two-pass algorithm (compute mean, then sum squared deviations). This confirms that Welford's method is numerically equivalent to the two-pass approach.

2. **The "updating formula":** The pairwise (parallel) algorithm for combining variances from two disjoint batches. Given batch A (size n_A, mean μ_A, variance σ_A²) and batch B (size n_B, mean μ_B, variance σ_B²), the combined variance can be computed in O(1) without re-scanning either batch.

3. **Comparison of naive vs. corrected algorithms:** They show that the naive formula `sum(x²) - n*mean²` can have large relative error when the variance is small relative to the mean.

## Relevance to iterflow

This paper is the foundational reference for the numerical stability properties of Welford-based streaming statistics. It provides the theoretical guarantee that iterflow's `streamingVariance()` is not just algorithmically convenient but provably accurate.

The paper is also the source for `streamingCovariance()` — the bivariate generalization of the pairwise updating formula is a standard extension of Chan et al.'s result.

## Limitations / caveats

- The paper focuses on floating-point error analysis, not streaming/online algorithms per se. Welford 1962 is still the primary citation for the online algorithm itself.
- No PDF available in Zotero; claims are based on established secondary references (Higham 2002 Chapter 1 cites and summarizes Chan et al. extensively).

## Key claims supported

- Numerical stability of iterflow's Welford-based operators is not just a practical property — it is theoretically bounded.
- Supports the paper's claim that the algorithm maintains only three numbers (count, mean, M2) with O(1) memory and provable accuracy.
